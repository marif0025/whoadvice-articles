# WhoAdvice: Good Writing Examples

All examples in this file are original. They demonstrate the project voice; they are not templates to repeat word-for-word.

## 1. Roundup introduction: skin care

> A body oil can leave dry skin looking smoother in minutes, but the finish varies more than product photos suggest. Some formulas sink in quickly and leave only a soft sheen. Others remain glossy, strongly scented, or noticeably shimmery long after application.
>
> We compared current ingredient lists, packaging, prices, and recurring customer feedback to find options for different preferences. Because WhoAdvice did not conduct hands-on testing for this guide, texture and scent observations are attributed to brands or reviewers. Our top picks prioritize moisturization, an even finish, and a drawback you can understand before buying.

Why it works:

- Starts with a practical distinction
- Explains the research method
- States the evidence limitation
- Previews the selection criteria

## 2. Roundup introduction: hair care

> Fine waves and tight coils rarely need the same curl cream. A rich formula can give coarse hair useful slip and moisture, yet leave looser patterns flat by midday. The better choice depends on how much conditioning, hold, and weight your hair can handle.
>
> For this guide, we compared formulas, expert guidance, retailer feedback, and discussions from people with different curl patterns. The picks are organized by need rather than by popularity alone.

Why it works:

- Rejects one-size-fits-all advice
- Uses concrete hair concerns
- Moves directly into methodology

## 3. Product review: skin care

> This fragrance-free moisturizer is our strongest pick for readers who want a simple daytime formula under sunscreen. The ingredient list combines glycerin with several emollients, while the pump keeps the product more protected than an open jar. Reviewers most often praise its quick absorption and lack of greasy residue.
>
> The formula is not especially rich. People with very dry skin frequently say they need a heavier cream at night, so this makes more sense as a light daily layer than as a complete answer for persistent flaking.

Why it works:

- Connects formula and packaging to use
- Attributes sensory feedback
- Includes a meaningful limitation
- Defines the best use case

## 4. Product review: hair care

> Where this cream stands out is moisture. Shea butter and plant oils give it a denser base than the lightweight stylers in this list, and users with coarse curls regularly mention better slip during twisting or braiding. The same richness is its main tradeoff: fine-haired reviewers are more likely to report buildup or reduced volume.
>
> Choose it for dry, dense curls when softness and definition matter more than an airy finish.

Why it works:

- Leads with the differentiator
- Connects the drawback to a user type
- Ends with a decisive recommendation

## 5. Product review: personal-care device

> The compact head is the main reason to choose this epilator. It should be easier to position around the knees, ankles, and underarms than the wider models we compared. The official manual also confirms wet and dry use, and the package includes a cleaning brush and sensitive-area cap.
>
> It is not the fastest option for both legs, and several reviewers describe the power button as stiff. For smaller areas or first-time users who value control, however, the slower pace may be an advantage rather than a flaw.

Why it works:

- Separates verified facts from editorial inference
- Explains the feature consequence
- Reframes a tradeoff for the right audience

## 6. Customer-review synthesis

> Recent reviews are most consistent on two points: the serum feels hydrating soon after application, and the dropper can be messy. Scent feedback is divided. Some buyers call it mild, while fragrance-sensitive users describe it as lingering. Because these are subjective reports rather than controlled test results, we treat them as guidance about the experience, not proof of performance.

Why it works:

- Reports both positive and negative patterns
- Recognizes subjectivity
- States the evidence limit without sounding legalistic

## 7. Reddit synthesis

> Reddit discussions add one useful caution that is easy to miss on retailer pages. Several users say the cream works better when they apply less than the label suggests, particularly on fine curls. That does not prove the product causes buildup, but it supports starting with a small amount and adding more only where needed.

Why it works:

- Uses Reddit for technique and edge cases
- Does not elevate anecdote into proof
- Produces practical guidance

## 8. Manufacturer claim

> The brand says the deodorant provides odor protection for up to 48 hours. We did not locate independent testing that confirms that duration, and customer feedback varies widely. Treat the 48-hour figure as a manufacturer claim rather than an expected result for every user.

Why it works:

- Preserves the claim
- Identifies the evidence gap
- Sets realistic expectations

## 9. Buying-guide explanation

> A larger epilator head can remove more hair with each pass, which is useful on the legs. It may be harder to control around curved or sensitive areas, though. A smaller head takes longer but gives you more precision. Neither size is automatically better; match it to the area you expect to treat most often.

Why it works:

- Explains both sides of a feature
- Avoids declaring a universal winner
- Supports a purchasing decision

## 10. Comparison verdict

> Choose Product A when you want more attachments and wet use. Choose Product B when battery life and a lighter handle matter more. Product A is the more versatile package, but Product B is the easier recommendation for frequent travelers.

Why it works:

- Gives direct user-based decisions
- Avoids “both are great”
- Distinguishes versatility from convenience

## 11. Informational introduction

> Frizz and breakage can look similar from a distance, but they call for different responses. Frizz often involves raised cuticles, dryness, or humidity. Breakage means the strand has weakened enough to split or snap. Before adding another smoothing product, it helps to work out which problem you are seeing.

Why it works:

- Clarifies a common confusion
- Gives a reason to keep reading
- Avoids diagnosis or alarm

## 12. FAQ answer

### Can people with sensitive skin use an epilator?

> Some can, but sensitivity varies and epilation can cause temporary redness or irritation. Follow the device instructions, avoid broken or already inflamed skin, and begin with a small area. Stop if you develop a severe or persistent reaction, and seek professional advice when needed.

Why it works:

- Answers immediately
- Includes sensible limits
- Avoids promising safety for everyone

## 13. Transparent evidence gap

> We could not confirm whether this model can operate while plugged in. Two retailer pages give conflicting information, and the current manual only describes cordless use. We have therefore treated it as cordless-only in the comparison.

Why it works:

- Does not hide uncertainty
- Explains the editorial decision
- Prevents a false specification

## 14. Value explanation

> This is not the cheapest bottle, but it is the better value among the concentrated formulas. At the price checked, each ounce costs less than the smaller premium pick, and reviewers commonly say a small amount covers the full face. Usage varies, so we have not converted that feedback into an exact cost per application.

Why it works:

- Explains value rather than equating it with low price
- Uses a concrete calculation
- Avoids false precision

## 15. Conclusion

> For most readers, Product A offers the best balance of control, useful attachments, and straightforward cleaning. Product B is the better choice for larger areas because its wider head should reduce the number of passes. Readers with very sensitive skin should prioritize the gentler settings and review the safety instructions before choosing either model.

Why it works:

- Restates the decision
- Names an alternative
- Preserves an important caveat

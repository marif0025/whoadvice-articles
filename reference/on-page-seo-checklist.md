# WhoAdvice On-Page SEO Checklist

Use this checklist for buying guides, product roundups, and supporting articles before publication and during content refreshes.

## 1. Search intent and page role

- [ ] Define one primary keyword and the reader's dominant intent.
- [ ] Confirm the current search results favor the planned page type, such as a ranked buying guide, tutorial, or individual review.
- [ ] State whether the page is a pillar guide or supporting article, and prevent keyword cannibalization with existing WhoAdvice pages.
- [ ] Identify the primary decision the page must help the reader make.
- [ ] Cover the useful subtopics readers expect without copying a competitor's outline.
- [ ] Record the target country, research date, and evidence model.

## 2. Keyword and topic map

- [ ] Assign one primary keyword.
- [ ] Collect natural variants, questions, attributes, body areas, mechanisms, brands, and comparison terms from search results and authoritative sources.
- [ ] Treat so-called “LSI keywords” as related terms and entities, not as a density target or a special Google technology.
- [ ] Map related terms to relevant sections before drafting.
- [ ] Use the primary keyword naturally in the title tag, H1, opening 100 words, at least one useful H2, meta description, and slug where practical.
- [ ] Use variants only where they clarify the topic; never force repetitions or target a keyword density.

## 3. Search snippet

- [ ] Write a unique title tag, normally about 50–60 characters, with the primary keyword near the beginning and a clear reason to click.
- [ ] Write one descriptive H1 that accurately represents the page; it may differ from the title tag.
- [ ] Write a unique meta description, normally about 140–160 characters, that includes the primary keyword, identifies the audience or benefit, and sets an honest expectation.
- [ ] Use a short, readable, hyphenated slug that describes the topic.
- [ ] Confirm the canonical URL matches the preferred live URL.

## 4. Opening and answer-first structure

- [ ] Prefer one interesting opening question when it naturally states the reader's real decision, boundary, misconception, or tradeoff; use a direct answer when that is clearer.
- [ ] When a question is used, begin answering it in the next sentence; do not use suspense, a question stack, or a generic curiosity hook.
- [ ] Use the primary keyword naturally within the first 100 words.
- [ ] Explain who the guide is for and what decision it resolves.
- [ ] Give the leading recommendation or direct answer early.
- [ ] Disclose affiliate relationships and the evidence model near the top when applicable.
- [ ] Avoid throat-clearing, generic market claims, and unsupported superlatives.
- [ ] Add a compact quick summary near the top when it helps a reader act faster; for roundups, use approved top-pick cards or a short decision summary rather than repeating the introduction and comparison table.

## 5. Heading hierarchy and readability

- [ ] Use one H1.
- [ ] Give each major section a descriptive H2.
- [ ] Use H3s only for subsections within the preceding H2.
- [ ] Keep headings short, specific, and useful when scanned on their own.
- [ ] Include the primary keyword in an H2 only where it reads naturally.
- [ ] Keep paragraphs focused and use tables or lists only when they improve a decision.
- [ ] Add a table of contents or jump links when the published page is long enough to benefit.

## 6. Buying-guide content quality

- [ ] Match the reader's likely budget, use case, experience level, and constraints.
- [ ] Use exact product names and model numbers.
- [ ] Explain why each product suits a specific user and when another pick is better.
- [ ] Cover meaningful drawbacks, intended-use limits, and safety constraints.
- [ ] Separate manufacturer claims from independently supported conclusions.
- [ ] Avoid implying hands-on testing unless WhoAdvice has documented testing.
- [ ] Make the comparison table use consistent, decision-relevant fields.
- [ ] Keep product summaries, verdicts, pros, and cons distinct rather than repetitive.
- [ ] Include cheap, mid-range, and premium choices when the market supports them.
- [ ] Refresh price tiers, availability, model status, links, and specifications before publication.
- [ ] Use at least one concrete, evidence-safe scenario when an abstract rule is difficult to apply; never invent first-person experience or an unverifiable anecdote.
- [ ] Make “who should buy this” explicit in each recommendation through the Summary, Verdict, badge, or an approved field. Do not add a repetitive heading to every card when the existing fields already answer it.
- [ ] Use decision boxes for high-friction choices with two or more branches, such as corded versus cordless or standard versus pole reach. Keep them concise and ensure the same decision remains available as normal HTML text.
- [ ] Move raw specifications to a comparison table or source record. Keep only specifications that change fit, safety, package cost, control, or performance expectations in narrative copy.
- [ ] Add comparison callouts where the reader could reasonably choose between close alternatives. State the condition that changes the winner instead of repeating both product summaries.
- [ ] Add mini tips only when they provide a concrete measurement, compatibility check, safety boundary, or publication-day check. Label them consistently and avoid decorative tip boxes.
- [ ] Build emotional connection through accurate recognition of cost, fatigue, uncertainty, inconvenience, or safety concerns and then give practical help. Do not claim shared feelings or manufacture personal experience.

## 7. Trust, sourcing, and editorial evidence

- [ ] Prefer manufacturer manuals, product pages, government guidance, medical organizations, standards bodies, and other primary sources.
- [ ] Source safety, performance, compatibility, warranty, and numerical claims.
- [ ] Add a methodology section that explains ranking factors and evidence limitations.
- [ ] Include author, reviewer, updated date, and editorial or affiliate disclosures in the published template.
- [ ] Check recalls and material safety issues for relevant personal-care devices.
- [ ] Remove or qualify claims that cannot be verified.

## 8. Internal and external links

- [ ] Link to relevant WhoAdvice supporting guides with descriptive, natural anchor text.
- [ ] Add links from relevant established pages back to the new page after publication.
- [ ] Avoid using the exact same anchor repeatedly across the site.
- [ ] Link externally when an authoritative source materially helps the reader verify or understand a claim.
- [ ] Confirm every link resolves to the intended page and does not redirect to an unrelated product.
- [ ] Do not add links merely to reach a quota.

## 9. Images and media

- [ ] Use an original or properly licensed lead image that matches the page topic.
- [ ] Give image files descriptive names.
- [ ] Write concise alt text that describes the image's purpose; include a keyword only when it is genuinely descriptive.
- [ ] Add product images only for the exact model shown.
- [ ] Set width and height to reduce layout shift, compress files, and use modern formats where supported.
- [ ] Keep important comparison information available as HTML text, not only inside an image.
- [ ] Record purposeful visual suggestions in the brief or publisher handoff: exact-model images, measurement diagrams, decision flows, comparison graphics, or process illustrations. Do not request a visual that merely decorates the page.
- [ ] For roundup type sections, add a generation-ready prompt for a useful type-card or decision graphic when the categories are easier to compare visually.
- [ ] For tip, technique, and safety articles, mark the exact insertion point and provide a generation-ready prompt only where an image clarifies an action, boundary, sequence, or manual symbol.
- [ ] Keep every decision, warning, and comparison available as HTML text; generated images must not carry essential meaning alone.
- [ ] When recommending free-stock services, link to the source and license, verify the individual asset at download time, and avoid stock that implies an exact product, technique, result, or medical permission.

## 10. Structured data and SERP eligibility

- [ ] Use Article or BlogPosting markup with accurate author, reviewer, dates, headline, and image data.
- [ ] Use ItemList markup when the implementation accurately represents a ranked list.
- [ ] Use Product or Review markup only when the visible page and editorial process meet Google's eligibility rules.
- [ ] Add BreadcrumbList markup when breadcrumbs are visible.
- [ ] Keep FAQ content useful to readers; do not assume FAQ rich results will appear.
- [ ] Validate structured data and ensure it matches visible content.

## 11. Technical and page experience

- [ ] Confirm the page is indexable, returns 200, and is not blocked by robots directives.
- [ ] Include the page in the XML sitemap and request indexing after a meaningful launch or refresh.
- [ ] Test the canonical, mobile layout, comparison-table scrolling, buttons, and sticky elements.
- [ ] Check Core Web Vitals and compress or lazy-load noncritical media.
- [ ] Fix broken links, heading errors, duplicate metadata, and accessibility problems.
- [ ] Confirm affiliate links use the site's approved disclosure and link attributes.

## 12. Final editorial audit

- [ ] Read the page once for search intent and once for a real buying decision.
- [ ] Confirm the title, H1, opening, table, product order, verdict, and conclusion agree.
- [ ] Search the draft for the primary keyword and remove awkward repetitions.
- [ ] Check spelling, exact model names, measurements, price tiers, and intended areas.
- [ ] Ensure every section adds new information.
- [ ] Record unresolved CMS tasks separately from completed article edits.
- [ ] Schedule a refresh based on product turnover and search-result volatility.
- [ ] Confirm quick summaries, decision boxes, callouts, tips, scenarios, and visuals are selective, nonrepetitive, evidence-safe, and useful on mobile.
- [ ] Confirm at least one meaningful callout earns its place by shortening a decision, comparison, action, warning, or compatibility check; do not add a decorative callout quota.
- [ ] When the opening uses a controlling question, confirm the section sequence and conclusion form one answer path.
- [ ] Confirm FAQs come from genuine buyer intent and resolve secondary purchase, compatibility, safety, use-case, or ownership questions not already answered clearly in the body.

## Source notes

This checklist adapts useful principles from the [Backlinko on-page SEO template](https://api.backlinko.com/app/uploads/2020/06/on-page-seo-template.pdf), the [Backlinko template guide](https://backlinko.com/templates/marketing/on-page-seo), and the [WooRank on-page SEO checklist](https://www.woorank.com/en/blog/on-page-seo-checklist). WhoAdvice adds product-review requirements for exact models, evidence labeling, intended use, safety, pricing, and editorial independence.

Some older recommendations in source checklists are intentionally not adopted as fixed rules. WhoAdvice does not use keyword-density targets, minimum word counts, arbitrary link quotas, text-to-HTML ratios, or social-share counts as article-level quality goals.

# WhoAdvice: Bad Writing Examples

These examples are intentionally poor. Each one shows a pattern that must be removed during editing.

## 1. Empty introduction

Bad:

> In today’s fast-paced world, taking care of your skin has never been more important. With so many products available, choosing the right one can feel overwhelming.

Problems:

- Generic
- Could introduce any product
- Adds no useful distinction
- Uses a banned opening pattern

Better direction:

Start with the specific conflict the reader must resolve, such as rich versus lightweight hydration.

## 2. Fake testing

Bad:

> We tested this serum for 30 days and loved how quickly it absorbed.

Problem:

No testing record exists.

Correct research-led version:

> Reviewers commonly describe the serum as quick to absorb. WhoAdvice did not conduct hands-on testing for this article.

## 3. Brand claim presented as fact

Bad:

> This cream provides 48 hours of hydration and repairs the skin barrier.

Problems:

- May repeat marketing as fact
- “Repairs” may be a strong structural or medical claim
- No evidence level is stated

Better:

> The brand says the cream provides up to 48 hours of hydration. Its ingredient list includes humectants and emollients commonly used in barrier-supporting formulas, but we did not locate independent evidence confirming the 48-hour claim.

## 4. Ingredient determinism

Bad:

> Because it contains niacinamide, this serum will clear acne and remove dark spots.

Problems:

- Presence does not prove concentration or outcome
- “Clear acne” is a treatment claim
- Guarantees an individual result

Better:

> Niacinamide appears in the ingredient list. Research on the ingredient is relevant to several skin concerns, but the finished formula’s effect depends on concentration, formulation, use, and individual response.

## 5. Review cherry-picking

Bad:

> One reviewer said this was the best device she had ever bought, proving that it works.

Problems:

- One anecdote
- No context
- Uses opinion as proof

Better:

> Positive reviews often mention the close result and simple cleanup. A recurring complaint concerns battery performance after several months, so long-term reliability is less clear.

## 6. Feature dump

Bad:

> This epilator has 40 tweezers, two speeds, an LED light, a cap, a pouch, a brush, wet and dry use, cordless power, and an ergonomic handle.

Problems:

- Reads like copied listing bullets
- No prioritization
- No reader benefit
- No drawback

Better:

> The 40-tweezer head and wet/dry design make this one of the more flexible full-leg options. An LED light helps reveal shorter hairs, although the larger head offers less precision around the ankles than the compact pick.

## 7. Empty praise

Bad:

> This high-quality product offers excellent performance and premium features at a great price.

Problems:

- Says nothing measurable
- No evidence
- Sounds promotional

Better:

> At the checked price, it includes two attachments that cost extra with the closest competitor. Its value depends on needing those attachments; the base performance is not independently tested.

## 8. Unsupported universal

Bad:

> It is completely painless and safe for all skin types.

Problems:

- Impossible universal
- Safety and pain vary
- Potentially harmful

Better:

> The brand positions the lowest setting for gentler use, but discomfort and irritation vary. Follow the manual and avoid using the device on broken or inflamed skin.

## 9. Repetitive product paragraphs

Bad:

> Product A is a great option for dry skin. Product B is a great option for sensitive skin. Product C is a great option for mature skin.

Problems:

- Identical template
- No evidence
- No comparison

Better direction:

Open each section with its genuine differentiator and explain the supporting evidence.

## 10. Keyword stuffing

Bad:

> The best curl creams help you find the best curl cream for curls, and our best curl creams guide includes the best curly hair cream products.

Problems:

- Unnatural
- Redundant
- Low trust
- No value

Better:

> The right formula depends on how much moisture, hold, and weight your curl pattern can tolerate.

## 11. Manufactured urgency

Bad:

> Run, don’t walk—this must-have deal will be gone before you know it!

Problems:

- Unverified urgency
- Sales language
- No product justification

Better:

> The price was 25% below the listed retail price when checked on June 28, 2026. Stock and discounts can change.

## 12. Competitor laundering

Bad:

> This is dermatologist approved because another buying guide says experts recommend it.

Problems:

- Vague source
- Changes “recommended” into “approved”
- No original verification

Better direction:

Locate the original expert statement or remove the claim.

## 13. Reddit as proof

Bad:

> Reddit users confirm that this oil grows hair faster.

Problems:

- Anecdotal
- Medical or biological claim
- No causation

Better:

> Some Reddit users associate the oil with improved hair appearance, but those anecdotes do not establish faster growth. We did not locate strong evidence supporting a growth claim for the finished product.

## 14. Meaningless con

Bad:

**Cons**
- May not be for everyone

Problem:

Every product may not be for everyone. The statement gives no decision help.

Better:

- Rich texture may flatten fine waves
- No fragrance-free option
- Replacement head costs nearly half the device price

## 15. Overloaded sensory language

Bad:

> This luxurious, silky, dreamy, indulgent cream melts effortlessly into thirsty skin for an instantly radiant, supple, glowing transformation.

Problems:

- Adjective pile
- Marketing tone
- Unsupported result
- No source

Better:

> Reviewers describe the cream as dense but easy to spread. Most positive feedback focuses on reduced tightness, while some users find the finish too glossy for daytime wear.

## 16. Generic conclusion

Bad:

> Ultimately, the perfect product depends on your unique needs and preferences. We hope this comprehensive guide helps you embark on your beauty journey.

Problems:

- Avoids a verdict
- Uses banned phrases
- Adds no final decision

Better:

> Product A is the strongest all-round choice for dry skin. Choose Product B instead when fragrance-free formulation matters more than richness.

## 17. Fake precision

Bad:

> Ninety percent of users experienced better hydration.

Problem:

No dataset supports the number.

Better:

> Hydration is the most common positive theme in the reviews we examined, but the reviews do not provide a controlled response rate.

## 18. “Clinically proven” without study details

Bad:

> This clinically proven formula reduces wrinkles.

Problems:

- No study
- No sample or outcome
- May overstate a cosmetic claim

Better:

> The brand describes the formula as clinically tested, but it does not provide enough public detail for us to evaluate the method or result.

## 19. Table/prose conflict

Bad table:

> Runtime: 60 minutes

Body:

> The battery lasts 40 minutes.

Problem:

The article is internally inconsistent.

Required action:

Resolve the source conflict before publication or mark the runtime as unverified in both places.

## 20. Hidden evidence gap

Bad:

> The product is waterproof.

Problem:

The source only says “washable head.”

Better:

> The head is washable, according to the manual. We found no confirmation that the full device is waterproof, so do not submerge it.

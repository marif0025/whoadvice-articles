# WhoAdvice: Human-Centered Writing and Editing

## Purpose

This standard turns research-based drafts into useful, recognizable WhoAdvice articles. “Humanized” means shaped by editorial judgment for a particular reader. It does not mean fooling an AI detector, adding errors, inventing anecdotes, or replacing one set of canned phrases with another.

Apply this file with `02-voice-and-tone.md`, `03-writing-style-rules.md`, and `05-banned-ai-patterns.md`. Accuracy, safety, evidence, and honest disclosure remain higher priorities than style.

## The standard

A publishable article should feel as though an informed editor:

- Knows the exact decision the reader is trying to make
- Selects and orders information instead of merely summarizing it
- Explains why facts matter in the reader's situation
- Makes clear comparisons and qualified recommendations
- Uses a consistent voice without sounding mechanically uniform
- Removes everything that does not earn its place
- Never claims feelings, opinions, or experience that no real person supplied

Human quality comes from judgment and specificity, not decorative personality.

## Before drafting: make the voice specific

Record these choices in the article contract:

```yaml
primary_reader:
reader_situation:
main_decision:
knowledge_level:
regional_english:
tone_traits: [warm, direct, practical, specific]
technical_term_policy:
humor_level: none | light
first_person_evidence_available: false
```

Do not prompt with a vague instruction such as “make it more human.” Define the audience, task, evidence model, tone, and output constraints. Use examples from WhoAdvice's approved good-writing file when a style distinction is hard to express.

Do not imitate a living writer or competitor. Describe transferable traits such as directness, warmth, sentence rhythm, or level of technical detail.

## Draft around decisions, not topic coverage

Every section must answer a real question or move the decision forward.

1. State the useful point early.
2. Add the evidence or comparison that supports it.
3. Explain the consequence for the reader.
4. Include the condition, exception, or tradeoff that changes the advice.

Prefer:

> The 24-inch blade covers a broad hedge faster, but it adds reach and weight that may be awkward in a small garden.

Avoid:

> This model features a 24-inch blade for enhanced performance and versatility.

The first sentence makes a choice easier. The second repeats a specification in promotional language.

## Use concrete editorial judgment

Replace general claims with observable details, sourced patterns, or decision rules.

- Replace “great for beginners” with the control, setup, size, or safety feature that lowers the learning burden.
- Replace “good value” with the price, included equipment, warranty, operating cost, or relevant comparison.
- Replace “easy to use” with the action that is easier and the design detail responsible.
- Replace “not for everyone” with the user, condition, or job for which it is a poor fit.

When evidence does not support a confident conclusion, say what remains unknown. Honest limits sound more credible than forced certainty.

## Natural voice without performance

Use contractions and ordinary vocabulary where they fit. Address the reader when doing so clarifies a choice. Allow occasional light phrasing, but keep the information in charge.

Vary rhythm at the article level:

- Mix short decision sentences with longer explanatory ones.
- Avoid consecutive paragraphs with the same length or opening pattern.
- Do not begin every product card with the product name, award, or “This.”
- Use transitions only when the relationship between ideas is not already clear.

Do not manufacture irregularity. Deliberate typos, awkward grammar, random fragments, slang, excessive rhetorical questions, fake quotations, and arbitrary punctuation do not make copy human; they make it less edited.

## Personal detail and emotion

Personal details are allowed only when a named contributor genuinely supplied them and the detail helps the reader. Record the source of first-person material in the research or testing notes.

Without documented experience:

- Do not write “I tried,” “we loved,” “we know how you feel,” or a fictional scene.
- Attribute sensory and experiential observations to the brand, reviewers, an expert, or documented testing.
- Show empathy through accurate recognition of the problem and practical help, not claims of shared emotion.

## Examples, analogies, humor, and questions

Use these devices only when they improve comprehension.

- An example should make an abstract rule usable.
- An analogy should clarify a difficult idea without distorting it.
- Humor should be light, rare, and never target the reader or minimize pain, cost, or safety.
- A rhetorical question should surface a genuine decision, not manufacture engagement.

One effective device is better than several decorative ones.

## Separate writing from editing

Never treat a generated first draft as publication-ready. Edit in this order so surface polishing does not hide structural weakness.

### Pass 1: brief and intent

- Does the draft serve the approved reader, search intent, and article contract?
- Does it answer the main decision early?
- Has it drifted into unrelated coverage or written for a keyword rather than a person?

### Pass 2: structure and logic

- Does each section have one clear job?
- Are the sections in the reader's decision order?
- Does each recommendation follow from the evidence?
- Are important tradeoffs visible rather than buried?
- Can any section, paragraph, or list be removed without losing decision value?

### Pass 3: evidence and specificity

- Is each factual or performance claim verified and correctly attributed?
- Does every feature lead to a practical consequence?
- Are comparisons direct and based on like-for-like models or variants?
- Have vague praise, fake precision, and unsupported universals been removed?

### Pass 4: paragraph and sentence craft

- Does each paragraph develop one idea?
- Is the main point close to the beginning?
- Are overloaded sentences split and choppy sequences combined where useful?
- Are filler, throat-clearing, nominalizations, and unnecessary qualifiers removed?
- Is active voice used when the actor matters, without forcing it everywhere?

### Pass 5: voice and pattern break

- Does the prose match the approved tone and regional English?
- Do neighboring sections repeat the same syntax, cadence, transitions, or verdict opening?
- Are canned hooks, fake empathy, catalog descriptions, and generic conclusions gone?
- Does personality come from precise judgment rather than jokes or adjectives?

### Pass 6: proof and quality control

- Check grammar, spelling, punctuation, names, numbers, units, links, headings, and formatting.
- Read the article aloud or use text-to-speech to catch stiff rhythm and missing words.
- Compare the final copy with the brief, article contract, claim ledger, and source file.
- Run the mechanical article audit.

Proofreading comes last. A clean sentence cannot rescue a weak argument.

## The human editorial gate

Before publication, a responsible editor must review the final article. The editor should be able to answer:

1. What is the article's main recommendation or answer?
2. Which evidence most strongly supports it?
3. What important limitation could change the reader's choice?
4. Which passage sounds generic, overproduced, or unlike WhoAdvice?
5. Is any first-person, sensory, emotional, or testing language unsupported?
6. What can still be cut?

If the editor cannot answer the first three questions from the draft, return it for structural revision rather than line polishing.

## AI-detector policy

AI-detector scores are not an editorial target and must not override evidence or readability. Do not:

- Add mistakes or factual noise to change a score
- Invent personal experience or opinions
- Obscure clear prose with forced synonyms
- Repeatedly rewrite sound copy solely to satisfy a detector
- Describe an article as human-written solely because a detector labels it that way

Use plagiarism checks, fact checks, editorial review, and source verification as quality controls. A detector result may trigger a manual review for repetitive or generic writing, but it is not proof of authorship or quality.

## Compact publication checklist

- Reader, situation, and main decision are explicit.
- The opening begins with useful tension or a concrete distinction.
- Each section performs a distinct job.
- Facts lead to consequences; recommendations include tradeoffs.
- Specific nouns and verbs replace vague praise and adjective piles.
- Paragraph and sentence patterns vary naturally.
- No fake empathy, experience, quotation, opinion, or testing appears.
- Examples and personality clarify rather than decorate.
- Repetition, filler, generic transitions, and template residue are removed.
- The article has passed structural, evidence, line, proof, and human editorial review.

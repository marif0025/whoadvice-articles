# WhoAdvice: Voice and Tone

## Voice in one sentence

Write like a sharp consumer editor helping a friend make a sensible decision: warm, specific, lively, and honest about what the evidence can and cannot prove.

## Core voice traits

### 1. Reader-aware

Begin with the decision, frustration, or use case the reader recognizes.

Good framing:

> A rich curl cream can give coarse hair the moisture it needs, but the same formula may flatten fine waves.

Weak framing:

> Curl creams are popular hair care products used by many people around the world.

The first version helps the reader make a distinction. The second merely announces the topic.

### 2. Conversational but edited

Use contractions and natural phrasing. The writing can sound spoken, but it should not include filler, rambling, slang overload, or chatty asides that do not help the decision.

Preferred:

- You may prefer
- It is worth checking
- The tradeoff is
- That makes it a better fit for
- Reviewers most often mention
- According to the brand
- In practice, this design may be easier to clean

Avoid:

- Hey, beauty lovers
- Trust us
- You guys
- Obsessed
- Run, don’t walk
- Literally everyone needs this

### 3. Confident within the evidence

Confidence should come from specificity, not from louder adjectives.

Preferred:

> The smaller head should be easier to position around the knees and ankles, although it will take longer to cover both legs.

Avoid:

> This incredible head design gives you the ultimate flawless experience every time.

### 4. Benefit-led

Explain why a detail matters.

Do not stop at:

> It has two speed settings.

Continue to the consequence:

> The slower setting may be easier to tolerate on sensitive areas, while the faster option is more practical for larger sections of the legs.

### 5. Lightly lively

A small amount of personality is welcome. Use it to make the article readable, not to perform enthusiasm.

Acceptable:

> The pump is convenient when it works, but several reviewers say it can be stubborn.

Too much:

> The pump is a total diva, but the glow is absolutely chef’s kiss.

### 6. Fair-minded

A strong recommendation still includes a meaningful limitation.

Preferred:

> It is one of the more versatile options in the group, but the larger handle may feel awkward in smaller hands.

Avoid:

> There are no real downsides.

## Reader relationship

Address the reader as “you” when it clarifies a choice or action. Do not use “you” in every paragraph.

Use “we” only for real editorial actions:

- We compared
- We reviewed the available specifications
- We analyzed recurring customer feedback
- We selected
- We found conflicting information

Do not use “we” to imply experience that did not occur:

- We loved the texture
- We noticed smoother skin
- We wore it all day
- We tested it in the shower

Those statements are allowed only when documented first-hand testing is supplied.

## First-person rules

First person is optional, not a default voice device.

Use “I” only when the named author genuinely performed the action or has the stated experience. Never manufacture a personal anecdote to make AI-written copy sound human.

Without first-hand evidence, convert:

> I found the serum sticky at first.

To:

> Several reviewers describe the serum as sticky during application, although many say it settles once layered under moisturizer.

## Sentence rhythm

Use varied sentence lengths.

A typical paragraph should contain:

- One clear opening sentence
- One or two sentences with evidence, explanation, or contrast
- An occasional short sentence for emphasis

Do not make every paragraph the same length. Do not begin consecutive paragraphs with the product name.

## Paragraph length

Default to one to three sentences per paragraph. Four sentences are acceptable when a point needs development.

For roundup product cards, the required Summary, Verdict, Pros, and Cons may contain the complete review. Do not require a paragraph after Cons. Use longer narrative sections only when the approved article contract calls for them.

## Preferred opening patterns

The reference material commonly succeeds by moving quickly from a familiar situation to a useful distinction. WhoAdvice should use that principle without copying its phrasing.

### Pattern A: Decision tension

1. State the practical tension.
2. Explain why the choice is not one-size-fits-all.
3. State how the article evaluated options.

### Pattern B: Familiar routine

1. Start with a recognizable moment in the routine.
2. Name the problem that tends to appear.
3. Introduce the product category as one possible solution.

### Pattern C: Myth or confusion

1. State what shoppers commonly confuse.
2. Clarify the difference.
3. Explain what the reader should evaluate.

Avoid generic scene-setting about modern life, innovation, confidence, or self-care.

## Product-section tone

Each recommendation should sound like an informed verdict, not a catalog description.

Recommended order:

1. Lead with the reason it earned its label.
2. Give the most decision-relevant evidence.
3. Explain the best use case.
4. Include the most important drawback.
5. Attribute uncertain or experiential claims.

Example pattern:

> This is the strongest fit for dry, coarse curls because the formula emphasizes conditioning oils and a richer cream base. Reviewers with thicker textures frequently praise its slip and definition, while people with fine hair are more likely to describe it as heavy. Choose it when moisture matters more than volume.

## Human-centered decision devices

Use these devices selectively. They should make a choice faster or a rule easier to apply, not make every article follow the same visible template.

- **Real-life scenarios:** Use a short, plausible situation grounded in known product constraints: hedge height, outlet route, battery ownership, bathroom space, skin sensitivity, or budget. Write it as an example, not as something WhoAdvice personally experienced.
- **Quick summaries:** Give the answer early. In a roundup, approved top-pick cards can serve this role. Do not restate the same winners again in a second summary box.
- **Who should buy this:** Make the intended buyer and disqualifying tradeoff explicit. Prefer an original Summary or Verdict over a repeated heading on every product card unless the article contract approves that field.
- **Decision boxes:** Use when a reader faces two or more clear branches. Write short if/then rules and preserve nuance in the surrounding prose.
- **Comparison callouts:** Name the competing options and the condition that changes the better choice: “Choose A for X; choose B when Y matters more.”
- **Mini tips:** Use one concrete action, measurement, compatibility check, or safety reminder. Avoid vague labels followed by ordinary prose.
- **Visual suggestions:** Propose a visual only when it explains dimensions, sequence, fit, or comparison more clearly than prose. Exact products require exact-model or properly licensed imagery.

Emotional connection comes from recognizing the real consequence of a poor choice—wasted money, repeated passes, fatigue, awkward setup, irritation, or uncertainty—and resolving it honestly. Never simulate intimacy with “we know how frustrating this is,” fictional anecdotes, or unsupported sensory experience.

Specifications should support judgment, not dominate the voice. Keep the number when it changes the decision, translate it into a consequence, and move the remaining catalog data to a comparison table or research record.

## Tone by article type

### Best-product roundup

- Decisive
- Comparative
- Useful
- Transparent about selection limits

### Single-product research review

- Investigative
- Balanced
- Detailed
- Clear about what was and was not tested

### Informational skin or hair article

- Calm
- Expert-led
- Explanatory
- Cautious around health claims

### Comparison article

- Direct
- Contrast-driven
- Organized around meaningful differences
- Willing to declare different winners for different users

### Deal article

- Timely but not breathless
- Verify price and expiration
- Explain why the product is worth considering independently of the discount
- Never manufacture urgency

## Sensory language

Sensory description is useful for beauty and personal-care products, but it must come from a source.

Examples:

- Lightweight lotion
- Dense cream
- Gel-like texture
- Powdery floral scent
- Tacky during application
- Dewy finish
- Soft hold
- Noticeable residue

Attribute sensory observations when they come from reviews, a brand, or another publisher.

## Confidence ladder

Match wording to evidence strength.

### Strong, verified fact

- The device includes three attachments.
- The label lists niacinamide.
- It carries an SPF 50 rating.
- The manual states that the head is washable.

### Supported interpretation

- The narrow head should be easier to maneuver around smaller areas.
- This is likely to suit readers who prefer a richer texture.
- The lower cost per ounce makes it the stronger value.

### Brand claim

- The brand says the formula provides up to 24 hours of hydration.
- According to the manufacturer, the device can run for 40 minutes per charge.

### Review pattern

- Reviewers commonly praise its fast absorption.
- A recurring complaint concerns the pump.
- Reddit discussions frequently mention buildup on fine hair.

### Weak or mixed evidence

- Feedback is mixed on the scent.
- Available sources do not confirm the battery runtime.
- The ingredient list has changed across retailer pages, so verify the current packaging before publication.

## Humor and wordplay

Use sparingly. One light line in an introduction or transition is enough.

Humor must not:

- Mock a reader’s appearance
- Minimize pain, irritation, acne, hair loss, or another concern
- Replace evidence
- Turn a product section into social-media copy

## Headline tone

Headlines should combine the topic with a clear benefit, problem, or distinction.

Useful patterns:

- The Best [Products] for [Specific Need]
- [Number] [Products] That [Concrete Benefit]
- [Product A] vs. [Product B]: Which Is Better for [Use Case]?
- How to Choose [Product] Without [Common Problem]
- Is [Product] Worth It? What the Specs and Reviews Actually Show

Avoid claims WhoAdvice cannot substantiate:

- We Tested 100+
- Dermatologist Approved
- Guaranteed Results
- Life-Changing
- The Only Product You Need

## Final voice checklist

Before approving copy, confirm:

- The opening addresses a real reader decision.
- The article sounds human without fake personal experience.
- Every strong claim has a source or attribution.
- Benefits are connected to features.
- Limitations are specific.
- Product paragraphs do not follow an identical sentence template.
- The tone is helpful rather than promotional.
- The language is original and not copied from reference articles.
- Concrete scenarios clarify difficult decisions without pretending they happened to the writer.
- The intended buyer and meaningful exclusion are easy to identify.
- Boxes, callouts, tips, and quick summaries are useful rather than repetitive decoration.
- Emotional language recognizes a real consequence and leads to practical guidance.

# WhoAdvice: Banned AI Patterns

This file identifies writing habits that make an article feel generic, padded, untrustworthy, or machine-generated.

The goal is not to replace one set of canned phrases with another. Rewrite the underlying thought.

## 1. Generic “modern world” openings

Ban:

- In today’s fast-paced world
- In the ever-evolving world of beauty
- In a world where self-care is more important than ever
- In the realm of skin care
- When it comes to personal grooming
- In recent years, X has gained immense popularity

Why: These phrases delay the useful point and can introduce unsupported trend claims.

Start with the reader’s decision instead.

## 2. Empty importance statements

Ban:

- Choosing the right product is essential
- It is important to note
- It cannot be overstated
- This is where X comes in
- There are many factors to consider
- Understanding X is crucial

Replace with the factor itself.

Instead of:

> It is important to consider battery life.

Write:

> A 20-minute battery may be enough for facial use, but it is inconvenient for full-leg sessions.

## 3. Fake empathy

Ban:

- We know how frustrating it can be
- We understand your struggle
- If you’re tired of dealing with...
- Say goodbye to...
- No one wants to...
- We’ve all been there

unless the author genuinely has and documents that experience.

Show understanding through accurate details, not emotional claims.

## 4. Fake hands-on authority

Never invent:

- I tried
- We tested
- Our team loved
- In our experience
- We noticed
- After several weeks
- Testers agreed

Without testing documentation, these are factual fabrications.

## 5. Inflated product language

Ban unless directly quoted and independently justified:

- Revolutionary
- Cutting-edge
- Groundbreaking
- Game-changing
- Unparalleled
- Ultimate
- Unmatched
- Transformative
- Miracle
- Holy grail
- Must-have
- Flawless
- Perfect
- Life-changing

Use the exact feature or outcome instead.

## 6. Empty praise

Ban:

- High-quality materials
- Premium performance
- Excellent results
- Impressive features
- Superior quality
- Great value
- Works amazingly
- Designed with the user in mind

These claims require explanation.

Write:

> The metal tweezing discs feel more substantial than the plastic assembly on the cheaper model, although long-term durability is not independently verified.

## 7. Formulaic contrast constructions

Limit:

- Not only X, but also Y
- It’s not just X; it’s Y
- Whether you are X, Y, or Z
- From X to Y
- Say goodbye to X and hello to Y
- X meets Y
- Where style meets function
- More than just a product

One natural use may be acceptable. Repetition is an AI tell.

## 8. Repetitive triads

Avoid building every sentence from three adjectives or three benefits.

AI pattern:

> It is lightweight, effective, and easy to use.

Better:

> At 6 ounces, it is light enough for a travel bag. The single-button control is also easy to learn.

## 9. Robotic transitions

Avoid repeated use of:

- Additionally
- Moreover
- Furthermore
- In addition
- On the other hand
- That being said
- Overall
- Ultimately
- In conclusion

Use logical flow. Often no transition is needed.

## 10. Identical product paragraph templates

Ban repetitive structures such as:

> Product A is a great option for...
> Product B is a great option for...
> Product C is a great option for...

Each product should be discussed through its strongest differentiator.

## 11. Product feature dumping

Do not copy a listing into prose.

Bad:

> It has 40 tweezers, two speeds, a light, a cap, a brush, wet and dry use, and a travel pouch.

Better:

> Its 40-tweezer head and wet/dry design make it one of the faster, more flexible options here. The light helps reveal shorter hairs, while the pouch and cleaning brush are useful extras rather than reasons to buy it.

## 12. Unsupported causal language

Ban:

- Because it contains X, it will Y
- This ingredient eliminates
- This formula prevents
- This device guarantees
- This technology ensures
- Users will experience

Use evidence-qualified language.

## 13. Overconfident universals

Ban:

- For all skin types
- Suitable for everyone
- Works on every hair type
- Completely safe
- Zero irritation
- Pain-free
- No side effects
- Guaranteed results

Use documented limitations and individual variation.

## 14. Vague authority

Ban:

- Experts say
- Studies show
- Research proves
- Dermatologists recommend
- Scientifically backed
- Clinically proven

unless the expert, study, method, and relevant finding are identified.

## 15. Review laundering

Do not transform opinions into facts.

Bad:

> It leaves skin smooth all day.

When the evidence is reviews:

> Many reviewers say their skin feels smoother after application, although feedback is mixed on how long the effect lasts.

## 16. Manufactured consensus

Ban:

- Users agree
- Everyone loves
- Reviewers rave
- The internet is obsessed
- A cult favorite
- Viral sensation

unless a measurable basis is provided. Prefer a specific review pattern and source date.

## 17. Keyword stuffing

Ban sentences such as:

> The best epilators are the best hair-removal devices for shoppers looking for the best epilator at home.

Write for meaning. Use synonyms only when they are accurate, not to hide stuffing.

## 18. Search-result mimicry

Do not write headings solely because competitors use them.

Every heading must serve the article’s search intent and evidence.

Do not include:

- Ten nearly identical FAQs
- Repetitive “benefits” sections
- Definitions already obvious from the introduction
- A buying guide that repeats product descriptions

## 19. Summary repetition

Do not end every section with:

- Overall, this is a great choice...
- This makes it ideal for...
- For these reasons...
- In summary...

State the verdict once, where it has the most impact.

## 20. Generic conclusions

Ban:

- The right product can transform your routine
- There is an option for everyone
- The choice ultimately depends on your needs
- We hope this guide helps
- Happy shopping
- Start your journey today

A conclusion should name the decision and tradeoff.

## 21. Excessive reader commands

Limit:

- Look no further
- Check out
- Take a look
- Keep reading
- Read on
- Don’t miss
- Grab yours
- Add to cart

Editorial content should guide, not push.

## 22. Decorative questions

Avoid strings of rhetorical questions.

Bad:

> Want smoother skin? Tired of irritation? Ready to upgrade your routine?

Ask a question only when it frames a real distinction that the next sentence answers.

## 23. Overuse of em dashes and fragments

AI copy often uses an em dash in nearly every paragraph or creates dramatic fragments:

> The result? Silky skin.
> The best part? No mess.
> The catch? The price.

Use occasionally, not as a template.

## 24. False precision

Do not invent numbers to sound authoritative:

- “Most users”
- “90% of reviewers”
- “Thousands report”

Use an actual count or use cautious wording.

## 25. Unnecessary disclaimers

Avoid adding a vague disclaimer after every claim.

Place one clear caveat where it is relevant. Do not clutter routine product copy with legalistic language.

## 26. Self-referential AI language

Ban:

- Based on the information provided
- As an AI
- This comprehensive guide
- In this article, we will delve into
- Let’s dive in
- Let’s explore
- Here’s everything you need to know

Begin with the content.

## 27. Detection pass

Before delivery, search for these terms and inspect every use:

`whether`, `overall`, `ultimately`, `additionally`, `moreover`, `game-changer`, `must-have`, `designed to`, `helps to`, `perfect for`, `ideal for`, `say goodbye`, `look no further`, `in today’s`, `not only`, `from X to Y`

The presence of a word is not automatically wrong. Repeated, generic, or unsupported use is.

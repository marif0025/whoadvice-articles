# WhoAdvice: Writing Style Rules

## 1. Use plain, precise English

Prefer familiar words unless a technical term is necessary.

Use:

- Helps reduce the look of dryness
- Feels heavy on fine hair
- Easy to rinse clean
- May irritate fragrance-sensitive skin
- Costs less per ounce

Avoid:

- Facilitates optimal moisturization
- Provides an unparalleled sensorial experience
- Utilizes advanced technological innovation
- Delivers transformative efficacy

Explain technical terms on first use.

> Humectants are ingredients that attract water, which can help a formula support skin hydration.

Do not define common words merely to add length.

## 2. Write for decisions, not word count

Every paragraph must perform at least one job:

- Explain
- Compare
- Qualify
- Verify
- Warn
- Recommend
- Attribute evidence
- Answer a likely question

Delete paragraphs that only repeat the heading or keyword.

## 3. Keep introductions focused

A roundup introduction should usually be 120–220 words.

It should contain:

1. The reader’s problem or decision
2. One useful distinction
3. The article’s research basis
4. A preview of how recommendations are organized

When the design includes separate top-pick cards, keep the introduction product-neutral. Do not name, rank, describe, or link individual products inline. Write original short descriptions for the top-pick cards in their own block immediately after the introduction. Do not reuse the introduction, comparison-table cells, product Summary, or Verdict verbatim.

Do not open with a dictionary definition, a sweeping statement about history, or a generic paragraph about confidence and self-care.

## 4. Build paragraphs around one idea

Preferred:

> The cream contains shea butter and several plant oils, giving it a richer base than the lightweight gels in this guide. That can be useful for coarse or very dry curls, but reviewers with fine hair frequently say they need only a small amount.

Avoid mixing formula, packaging, price, company history, color options, and four unrelated benefits in one paragraph.

## 5. Vary sentence structure

Avoid a sequence of identical product paragraphs:

> This product features...
> This product features...
> This product features...

Rotate natural entry points:

- The strongest reason to choose it is...
- Where this model stands out is...
- Its compact head makes more sense for...
- Review feedback is most positive about...
- Compared with the other options...
- The tradeoff is...

Variation must remain natural. Do not use a thesaurus to force novelty.

## 6. Use contractions

Use contractions where they sound natural:

- It’s
- You’ll
- Don’t
- Isn’t
- Won’t

Avoid contractions in formal warnings when clarity is more important.

## 7. Punctuation

- Use the Oxford comma.
- Use em dashes sparingly, no more than about one per 250 words.
- Do not use semicolons repeatedly.
- Avoid exclamation marks in editorial copy.
- Do not use ellipses for drama.
- Parentheses should add brief context, not hide an entire second paragraph.
- Use straight, readable sentence construction before adding stylistic punctuation.

## 8. Numbers and units

- Use numerals for product specifications, measurements, ratings, prices, percentages, and ages.
- Include a space between a number and most abbreviated units when normal editorial convention requires it: 40 minutes, 3 speeds, 1.7 fl. oz.
- Preserve official model numbers exactly.
- Standardize units within comparison tables.
- Show both ounces and milliliters only when it improves comparison.
- Do not invent conversions when the source is unclear.
- Add a date to prices and discounts because they change.

## 9. Product names

On first mention, use the verified full product name. Afterward, use a shorter unambiguous form.

Do not:

- Change a model number
- Combine two variants into one
- Assume an Amazon title is the official product name
- Treat a bundle as the base model
- Mix specifications from different generations

## 10. Ingredient language

Use the current ingredient list from the brand or packaging when available.

Do not infer:

- Ingredient concentration from list order alone
- Clinical efficacy from the presence of one ingredient
- Suitability for every skin type
- That “natural” means safer
- That an ingredient is harmful because a database assigns it a simplistic score

Preferred:

> Niacinamide appears in the ingredient list, but the brand does not disclose its concentration.

Avoid:

> The niacinamide guarantees brighter skin.

## 11. Claims and attribution

Place attribution next to the claim.

Preferred:

> The brand says the battery runs for up to 50 minutes.

Avoid:

> The battery runs for 50 minutes. According to the brand, this product is effective.

The first version makes the evidence status immediately clear.

## 12. Quotes

Use quotes only when the exact wording adds value.

- Keep quotes short.
- Identify the speaker and qualification.
- Do not quote retailer reviews at length.
- Paraphrase patterns across reviews instead of stitching together isolated praise.
- Never change wording inside quotation marks.
- Do not create composite or synthetic quotes.

## 13. Customer-review synthesis

A review section should identify patterns, not cherry-pick comments.

Recommended structure:

1. State the dominant positive pattern.
2. State the dominant complaint.
3. Identify which user type appears most affected.
4. Mention the review source and review count/date when available.
5. Avoid treating ratings as controlled evidence.

Example:

> Across recent retailer reviews, buyers most often praise the close shave and easy cleanup. The repeated complaint is that the replacement heads are expensive, particularly among people who use the device several times a week.

## 14. Reddit synthesis

Use Reddit to understand language, frustrations, edge cases, and long-term experiences.

Write:

> In several hair-care discussions, users with fine curls describe needing less product than the label suggests to avoid buildup.

Do not write:

> Reddit proves this product causes buildup.

Anecdotes can reveal a pattern worth investigating; they cannot establish universal causation.

## 15. Pros and cons

Pros and cons must be:

- Specific
- Evidence-based
- Different from one another
- Relevant to the purchasing decision
- Written in parallel form

Good:

**Pros**
- Narrow head is easier to position around the ankles
- Includes a washable cap
- Lower cost per ounce than most picks

**Cons**
- No confirmed battery indicator
- Several reviewers report a stiff power button

Weak:

**Pros**
- Amazing
- High quality
- Works well

**Cons**
- Could be better
- Not for everyone

Do not repeat a pro or con word-for-word in the body.

## 16. Headings

Headings should tell the reader what the section will resolve.

Good:

- How we selected the products
- What to look for in a curl cream
- Best for coarse, dry curls
- Where the cheaper model falls short
- What reviewers say about irritation

Weak:

- Overview
- Information
- Product details
- Other things
- Final thoughts

Default heading style:

- Article title: title case
- H2 and H3 headings: sentence case
- Product award labels: title case when used as a UI label

## 17. SEO integration

Use the primary keyword where natural:

- Title
- Introduction
- One relevant heading
- Buying guide or comparison context
- Conclusion when useful

Use related terms according to meaning, not to meet a quota.

Avoid:

- Repeating the exact primary keyword in every product section
- Awkward singular/plural grammar
- Adding “best” before every product name
- Writing sentences whose only purpose is keyword placement
- Using unrelated entities to increase topical breadth

The article must read naturally without the keyword list visible.

## 18. Internal links

Add an internal link when it genuinely helps the next decision.

Use descriptive anchor text:

- how to prevent razor burn
- our guide to epilating sensitive areas
- the difference between mineral and chemical sunscreen

Avoid:

- click here
- read more
- this article
- exact-match anchors repeated several times

## 19. Comparison language

Use direct contrasts:

- A is lighter; B is more conditioning.
- A offers more attachments; B is easier to clean.
- A costs less upfront; B has lower replacement costs.
- A better suits beginners; B gives experienced users more control.

Avoid vague comparisons such as “both are great choices.”

## 20. Conclusions

A conclusion should not summarize every section.

It should:

1. Restate the main decision
2. Name the strongest recommendation and its use case
3. Mention an alternative for a distinct need
4. Repeat a crucial caveat when necessary

Keep most roundup conclusions to 80–150 words.

Do not end with:

- The choice is yours
- There is something for everyone
- We hope this guide helped
- Embark on your journey
- Happy shopping

## 21. FAQ answers

Answer the question in the first sentence. Add context afterward.

Preferred:

> A curl cream can be used on wavy hair, but lightweight formulas are usually easier to apply without flattening the pattern.

Avoid:

> When it comes to the fascinating world of curl creams, there are several factors to consider before answering this important question.

## 22. Readability target

Aim for:

- Mostly 12–24 words per sentence
- Occasional shorter or longer sentences
- Short paragraphs
- Concrete nouns and verbs
- Minimal jargon
- No unexplained acronyms

Readability must not come at the expense of accuracy.

### Consumer-language pass

Before finalizing a draft:

- Split sentences over 30 words unless the structure remains unusually clear.
- Let tables carry specifications; use prose to explain consequences, fit, and tradeoffs.
- Replace internal research language such as exact-model evidence, manufacturer trail, no assumed credit, or scraping-status wording with plain consumer language.
- Use the target market's English consistently. For US articles, prefer extension cord, power cord, all-around, and electrical outlet.
- Hyphenate measurements used before nouns, such as 4.0-amp motor and 22-inch blade.
- Vary product verdict openings. Do not begin more than two cards with the same command verb.
- Add a concrete use example or decision rule when a product type or buying factor remains abstract.
- Prefer an actionable safety instruction before its supporting product detail.

## 23. Final style audit

Search the draft for:

- Repeated opening words
- Repeated sentence templates
- Exact-match keyword clusters
- Empty adjectives
- Unsupported superlatives
- Fake first-person statements
- Long paragraphs
- Excessive em dashes
- Generic transitions
- Repeated conclusions after every section

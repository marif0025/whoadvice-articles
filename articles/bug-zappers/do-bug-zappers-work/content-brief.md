## Working search package

- **Working title:** Do Bug Zappers Work? What They Kill—and What They Don’t Control
- **Meta direction:** Explain when electric grids and indoor light traps can kill or capture insects, why conventional UV zappers do not reliably reduce mosquito bites, and what to use instead.
- **Direct-answer target:** First 80–120 words.
- **Secondary families:** do bug zappers work on mosquitoes; flies; gnats; do bug zappers kill bees/beneficial insects; how bug zappers work; bug zapper vs mosquito trap.

The US SERP/title decision and subsequent review gates are consolidated in `audit.md`. The H1 is retained; the article contract uses a shorter title tag.

## Reader promise

Give a clear outcome-specific answer, then help the reader match the likely insect, environment, and control goal to the right mechanism. The page must explain that an insect being killed or captured is not the same outcome as fewer bites, a reduced population, or disease protection.

## Required opening answer

State, without suspense:

- Electric-grid zappers can kill insects that contact the grid.
- Conventional outdoor UV-only zappers have not been shown to reduce mosquito biting rates.
- Results for flies and small indoor flying insects depend on species, breeding source, placement, competing light, and capture mechanism.
- Outdoor devices can kill non-target insects.

Do not mention a product, award, retailer, or affiliate link in the opening.

## Required structure

### H1: Do Bug Zappers Work? What They Kill—and What They Don’t Control

1. **Short answer**
   - Direct four-part conclusion above.
   - Define “work” as a requested outcome, not just a visible zap.

2. **How bug zappers and insect traps differ**
   - Use the controlled taxonomy.
   - Explain open/guarded grids, enclosed indoor electric traps, UV glue boards, fans, solar grid power, handheld rackets, and lure-assisted mosquito traps such as CO2-, heat-, odor-, or octenol-assisted systems.
   - Keep “solar” clearly a power-source modifier.

3. **Do bug zappers work on mosquitoes?**
   - Apply all eight endpoints: attraction, device entry/contact, kill/capture, nearby abundance, human landing rate, bite rate, population reduction, and disease-risk reduction.
   - Assign each endpoint one precise status: evidence supports an effect, evidence supports no meaningful effect, not established, not evaluated, or cannot be inferred from the preceding endpoint.
   - Describe disease-risk reduction as not evaluated/not established and not inferable from kills—never as “disproved.”
   - State the conventional UV-only evidence boundary.
   - Briefly explain other mosquito cues and why lure-assisted systems require separate evidence.

4. **Do bug zappers work on flies?**
   - Distinguish house/nuisance flies from biting flies, then address species and indoor/outdoor context.
   - Sanitation, exclusion, and source control.
   - State: “Do not place an open-grid electrocuting trap near exposed food or food-preparation areas.” Cite current Texas A&M guidance and the peer-reviewed fragment-release study inline.
   - Describe non-electrocuting categories narrowly: “Glue-board traps and fan-only traps do not use the same electrocution mechanism.”

5. **Do bug zappers work on gnats?**
   - Explain that “gnat” is imprecise.
   - Distinguish at least fungus gnats, drain/moth flies, and fruit/vinegar flies by likely source without becoming a full identification guide.
   - Route toward source management; a trap may capture adults without resolving breeding.

6. **What about midges, moths, bees, and wasps?**
   - Distinguish non-biting midges from mosquitoes and moths from small flies.
   - Treat bees and wasps as distinct groups, not generic “flying bugs.”
   - Explain that incidental killing does not prove a device is an appropriate control choice.
   - Give each insect group its own suitable university, extension, government, or primary source; do not use the general UC IPM flies page as blanket evidence.

7. **Can bug zappers kill beneficial insects?**
   - Explain outdoor non-target kill and why “eco-friendly” is not a default claim.
   - Avoid a dramatic percentage unless the original study is checked and contextualized.

8. **Bug zapper vs mosquito trap**
   - Substantial section, not a standalone page yet.
   - Compare UV-only grid action with CO2/heat/odor/octenol and capture method.
   - Do not declare a universal winner. Conventional outdoor UV-only zappers have not been shown to reduce mosquito biting rates. Lure-assisted traps require exact-system field evidence, and a larger catch does not by itself establish fewer landings, fewer bites, or a smaller local population.

9. **What helps reduce mosquito bites?**
   - CDC-aligned source reduction, barriers/screens, clothing, and EPA-registered repellents.
   - Introduce the mosquito-killer alternatives page only after its repositioned contract is approved.

10. **How to decide**
   - Identify insect/source.
   - Define the desired outcome.
   - Choose indoor/outdoor and active/passive format.
   - Check exact manual, placement, maintenance, and environmental limits.
   - Set an evidence-appropriate expectation.

11. **FAQ**
    - Do bug zappers attract more mosquitoes to your yard? Retain and answer: “A conventional UV light may draw some nearby insects toward the device, and individual mosquitoes may approach it. The evidence does not establish that this increases mosquitoes across the yard. Conventional outdoor UV-only zappers also have not been shown to reduce biting rates.”
    - Can a bug zapper make a patio mosquito-free?
    - Are indoor UV traps useful for flies?
    - Will a bug zapper solve fungus gnats in houseplants?
    - Is an EPA establishment number an approval?

Only retain questions that the final SERP review and source base can answer accurately.

## Evidence and claims contract

- Cite university extension, CDC, EPA, or peer-reviewed evidence for insect behavior, mosquito outcomes, non-target effects, safety, and public-health guidance.
- Attribute manufacturer product statements; they do not establish category efficacy.
- Use the outcome ladder in `research.md` to stop attraction/catch evidence from becoming population/bite claims.
- Preserve the research packet's endpoint evidence status; do not simplify “not established” or “not evaluated” into “disproved.”
- Explain that EPA establishment numbers do not mean EPA safety or efficacy approval.
- Do not use “bug-free,” “mosquito-free,” “eco-friendly,” “non-toxic,” “humane,” “safe for children/pets,” “whole-yard protection,” or disease-risk language without directly applicable evidence; these claims are not expected to be necessary here.
- Do not turn advertised acreage, voltage, wattage, UV wavelength, or catch count into a performance conclusion.
- Do not publish a national beneficial-insect mortality estimate unless the original study and its extrapolation have been directly reviewed and recorded. No such number is required by this contract.
- Do not give a universal cleaning procedure; exact-model cleaning belongs in a later manual-first support workflow.

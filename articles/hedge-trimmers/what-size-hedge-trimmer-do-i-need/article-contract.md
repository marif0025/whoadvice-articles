## Blade length versus cutting capacity contract

Answer the distinction before introducing supporting terminology:

- **Blade length:** a lengthwise blade specification that affects coverage, maneuverability, leverage, storage, and number of passes.
- **Manufacturer-stated cutting capacity:** an exact-model claim about the growth the manufacturer says the tool can accept or cut, subject to its wording and conditions.

Then explain only as needed:

- **Cutting area:** may be shorter than the nominal blade length under a manufacturer's measurement convention.
- **Tooth gap, tooth spacing, blade gap, or tooth opening:** manufacturer terms for blade geometry that must be preserved rather than silently converted into cutting capacity.
- **Working reach:** may refer to actual tool dimensions or an advertised reach that includes operator assumptions; it is not interchangeable with blade length.

Do not imply that every manufacturer defines these fields identically. Do not let the terminology section exceed the space needed to make the buying distinction clear.

## Measurement section contract

Use the binding six-step order and make each step actionable.

### 1. Measure the hedge face

- Record the length and width of the broad faces or tops that must be covered.
- Explain that more straight surface can make extra blade coverage useful.
- Do not convert yard size into blade length.

### 2. Identify tight or detailed areas

- Note topiary, corners, narrow gaps, fences, walls, trunks, and direction changes.
- Explain why placement, handle control, and lower working weight may matter more than maximum blade length.

### 3. Check the highest and farthest cutting points

- Measure from stable ground and record whether each surface can be seen and controlled.
- Separate actual tool or shaft length from user-inclusive advertised reach.
- Do not recommend a ladder workaround.

### 4. Measure representative stems

- Check several stems, including established wood beneath soft outer growth.
- Record diameter and condition rather than using informal comparisons such as `finger-thick`.
- Explain that green, dry, hard, dead, and old wood may not behave alike at the same diameter.

### 5. Compare loaded tool weight

- For cordless tools, prefer an official battery-installed weight or a transparent calculation from compatible official components.
- For pole tools, include the complete working configuration where published.
- State that weight alone does not establish balance or fatigue.

### 6. Select blade length and capacity separately

- Choose blade length from coverage and control.
- Choose the exact model's stated capacity from representative growth. Avoid choosing a tool whose maximum stated capacity merely matches the thickest stems expected regularly; do not invent a percentage margin.
- Route isolated established woody stems to the companion tool-selection guide rather than expanding the pruning-tool explanation here.

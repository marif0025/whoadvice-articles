## Blade length versus cutting capacity

Blade length is the lengthwise blade specification. It affects coverage, placement, leverage, and storage. A longer blade can suit a broad, straight surface; a shorter one may place more easily around a shaped shrub.

The manufacturer's stated cutting capacity answers a different question. It is the exact brand's claim about the size of growth a particular model is designed to accept or cut. Treat an “up to” figure as a ceiling rather than a promise that every stem at that diameter will cut equally well.

Brands may instead publish tooth gap, tooth spacing, blade gap, or tooth opening. Preserve the exact documentation wording rather than assuming every gap measurement equals reliable capacity.

Nominal blade length may not equal the active cutting area under every manufacturer's measurement method. Working reach is different too: an advertised reach can include assumptions about the operator rather than describing the physical tool alone.

## What to measure before choosing a hedge trimmer

### 1. Measure the hedge face

Measure the length and width of each broad face or top. Long, straight surfaces increase the value of blade coverage because fewer passes may be needed.

Also record whether you can approach both sides. A wide hedge accessible from two sides presents a different reach problem from one beside a wall or fence.

### 2. Identify tight or detailed areas

Mark compact shrubs, topiary, corners, narrow gaps, fences, walls, and frequent direction changes. These areas reward accurate placement. Extra blade length may help on an open face but become awkward when the work repeatedly changes direction.

Consider the finest work as well as the largest face. One tool may need to sacrifice some coverage to remain manageable in restricted areas.

### 3. Check the highest and farthest cutting points

Measure surfaces you cannot see and control from stable ground, including the far edge of a wide top. Access from a slope, terrace, or opposite side can change the answer.

Do not add blade length to your height and call the result safe working reach. Compare actual tool or shaft dimensions, head angle, visibility, and the manual's required working position.

### 4. Measure representative stems

Check several stems instead of only soft tips. Look beneath new growth for older wood. Record diameter and whether stems are green, hard, dry, dead, tangled, or difficult to approach.

Equal diameters do not guarantee equal cutting effort. Species, age, moisture, position, blade condition, and the exact tool can all affect the result.

### 5. Compare loaded tool weight

Use the tool's working weight. For cordless models, that normally means the selected battery installed. For pole models, check the complete configuration and account for leverage from the distant head.

Weight alone does not prove balance or comfort. Battery position, blade length, handles, head angle, and the height of the cut also affect control.

### 6. Select blade length and capacity separately

Choose blade length from required coverage and control. Then compare manufacturer-stated cutting capacity with representative growth. Avoid a tool whose maximum merely matches the thickest stems expected regularly, but do not invent a fixed safety percentage.

## How to choose a shorter or longer blade

<callout>

An 18-inch blade may be easier to control around small shrubs and tight spaces, while a 22-inch blade may reduce passes on longer, straighter hedge faces. Loaded weight and handle design can reverse the apparent advantage.

</callout>

Choose more coverage when most of the work consists of open, straight faces and you can control the complete tool through the required angles. Choose more maneuverability when the hedge has curves, corners, obstacles, or detailed shapes. Storage length and the space available to turn the tool also matter.

Avoid looking for a universal “middle-length” blade. This page has no fixed product set that would define the middle. Choose a blade that balances coverage with the level of control your hedge requires.

Blade length still does not establish branch capacity. A 22-inch blade may cover more surface than an 18-inch blade while having a lower, equal, or higher manufacturer-stated capacity depending on the exact model.

## How stem thickness affects the choice

Measure the growth the trimmer will meet during ordinary maintenance, then check the exact model's published capacity language. Treat that figure as a manufacturer claim and upper limit. Do not infer it from blade length, voltage, amperage, motor type, or tooth geometry alone.

[University-extension pruning guidance](https://extension.umn.edu/planting-and-growing-guides/pruning-trees-and-shrubs) separates hedge shearing from selective cuts on larger woody growth. Fine growth may hide established stems that need selective removal. Dry or old wood can be a poor match even when its diameter appears to fit a stated maximum.

Do not turn tooth gap into a clean-cut guarantee. If the manufacturer publishes tooth spacing and cutting capacity separately, keep them separate. If it uses ambiguous wording, preserve that wording and avoid claiming more than the documentation supports.

## When a pole hedge trimmer makes sense

<callout>

Choose pole format because the hedge surface cannot be safely seen and controlled from stable ground—not simply because the hedge is tall. A tall hedge may be approachable from a terrace, slope, or both sides. A lower but very wide hedge may create a far-edge problem that a standard tool cannot solve cleanly.

</callout>

Compare actual tool dimensions with advertised reach, which may include an assumed operator position. Check shaft adjustment, head articulation, and whether you can see the cutting area.

Loaded working weight matters more as the cutting head moves away from the body. Extra reach increases leverage and can make a pole tool awkward on low shrubs. One pole model may cover several heights, but adjustable length does not remove the need for control at each position.

If fine hedge growth at height is the correct job, compare the [best pole hedge trimmers for tall hedges](/garden-tools/best-pole-hedge-trimmers/). Do not use a pole hedge trimmer as a pole saw for woody limbs. Avoid overhead utility lines, unstable access, and work that cannot be controlled from the ground; those conditions require qualified help rather than a longer reach claim.

## Common sizing mistakes

- **Choosing from yard size:** Measure the actual hedge faces, detailed areas, access, and stems instead.
- **Assuming a longer blade cuts thicker growth:** Select coverage and capacity as separate specifications.
- **Treating tooth gap as cutting performance:** Preserve the manufacturer's wording and do not turn geometry into a guarantee.
- **Measuring only soft tips:** Check representative established stems beneath the outer growth.
- **Comparing bare and loaded weights:** Use complete working configurations wherever reliable figures exist.
- **Reading maximum reach as shaft length:** Check physical dimensions and the assumptions behind any advertised reach.
- **Using blade length as a ladder substitute:** Move to a controlled pole format or qualified help rather than improvising access.
- **Forcing old woody stems through a trimmer:** Separate them and use the companion pruning-tool guide.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is an 18-inch or 22-inch hedge trimmer better for a small yard?

Yard size alone does not decide. An 18-inch blade may be easier around compact shrubs and tight spaces. A 22-inch blade may reduce passes on a longer, straight face. Compare hedge shape, loaded weight, handle design, and positioning room.

### Does a longer hedge-trimmer blade cut thicker branches?

No, blade length does not determine branch capacity. It mainly changes coverage, placement, leverage, and storage length. Check the exact model's manufacturer-stated cutting capacity separately.

### What does a hedge trimmer's cutting-capacity rating actually mean?

It is the manufacturer's exact-model claim about the maximum growth the tool is designed to accept or cut. An “up to” rating is not a universal clean-cut guarantee across green, hard, dry, dead, or old wood. Established woody stems may require loppers or a pruning saw.

### How should I measure hedge stems before buying?

Measure several representative stems, including established wood beneath the soft outer growth. Record the diameter and condition rather than relying on an informal comparison. Do not choose from the single softest or thickest visible tip.

### Do I need a pole trimmer for a 10- or 12-foot hedge?

Choose pole format only when the required surface cannot be safely seen and controlled from stable ground with a standard tool. Hedge height alone is not enough; access from both sides, slopes, terraces, face depth, shaft dimensions, head angle, and loaded weight can change the answer.

### Is a shorter blade easier to control?

A shorter blade may be easier to position around corners and detailed shapes, but blade length alone does not establish control. Loaded weight, balance, handles, tool layout, and cutting angle also matter.

### Can one pole hedge trimmer handle both low shrubs and tall hedges?

It can cover both only when its minimum and maximum working lengths, head positions, loaded weight, and balance remain manageable at each height. A fixed or extended shaft that helps on tall tops may feel awkward near low shrubs.

## Which hedge-trimmer size fits your hedge?

Choose blade length for coverage and maneuverability, then check manufacturer-stated cutting capacity as a separate exact-model claim. Measure the hedge face, detailed areas, highest and farthest surfaces, representative stems, and loaded tool weight before deciding. An 18-inch blade may favor placement while a 22-inch blade may reduce passes, but neither is a universal answer. Move to pole format when the cutting surface cannot be seen and controlled from stable ground. Route established woody stems to the companion lopper and pruning-saw guide instead of forcing the trimmer to do every job.
